# Competitor Landscape
## Corporate Food Services — Mexico

### Purpose of this Document
This document provides a competitor-specific analysis of the Mexican corporate food services market.
It identifies the real competitors Zitrone is compared against and how they actually position and message.

It includes:
- Named competitors (not just archetypes)
- Observable positioning logic and messaging patterns
- What each competitor is truly selling (buyer psychology)
- Where each wins/loses decisions
- White-space implications for Zitrone

---

## 1) Category Reality: How Buyers Create a Shortlist
Shortlists in Mexico typically form from:
- “Safe incumbents” (global/institutional operators)
- “Industrial powerhouses” (Mexico-wide operators with massive capacity)
- “Regional relationship operators” (mid-size, local strength)
- “Experience-first niche players” (office-focused, premium feel)

Buyers choose to minimize personal risk first, then optimize quality/cost/experience.
The default competitor is often “status quo” plus whichever provider procurement already trusts.

### 3) What They’re Actually Selling

# 6) What Competitors Converge On (Why Buyers Can’t Decide)
Across the competitive set above, the dominant messaging clusters are:

1) Scale and years (60+ years, national coverage, millions of meals)  
2) Safety, standards, certifications (hygiene, compliance posture)  
3) “Customization” as a vague promise  
4) “Experience” as a generic claim (rarely operationalized)

This creates:
- High message overlap
- Low memorability
- Procurement-first commodity dynamics
- Status quo bias: buyers keep current providers because switching feels risky

---

# 7) Strategic White Space (What No Major Competitor Owns Clearly)
No major competitor above clearly owns, in plain buyer language:

- Decision clarity (“where do we start, what model fits us?”)
- Transition confidence (how switching is de-risked)
- Risk transparency (what could go wrong, and how it’s prevented with evidence)
- Experience ownership (a system, not a list of services)
- Human accountability (who owns outcomes end-to-end)

---

# 8) Implication for Zitrone (How to Win vs Each Competitor)
Zitrone should not try to outscale Kosmos or out-institutionalize Sodexo.

Zitrone can win by:
1) **Designing the decision** (problem-first, scenario-first entry points)
2) **Owning transition** (explicit onboarding + governance model)
3) **Making risk legible** (controls translated into confidence, not jargon)
4) **System framing** (workplace food ecosystem, not “services list”)
5) **Human accountability** (named ownership, fast adaptation, measurable outcomes)

In this market, clarity beats scale.
The operator who makes the decision safest and simplest wins.
